Why a hybrid design

Combining TOP-Nav, ABS, and a learned navigation policy

Your supervisor wants RL genuinely present in the navigation layer, not just in locomotion underneath it. TOP-Nav alone doesn't satisfy that — its navigation decision is a classical planner, full stop, no learned policy anywhere above locomotion. ABS does have RL in navigation, but its dual agile/recovery policy structure is built for a narrower problem (collision avoidance via switching), without TOP-Nav's terrain-awareness or its closed-loop correction idea.

The design below takes the structural idea each paper is built around — not the whole paper — and combines exactly three of them into one system. Nothing here is invented from nothing; every piece traces to a specific paper, and the prose says explicitly which piece comes from where so you can defend each design choice individually if asked.

Be honest about scope

This is a real increase in complexity over your current single-policy design — two navigation policies instead of one, plus the switching logic between them. Read this whole document before deciding to build it; the recommended build order section at the end argues for getting your existing single-policy version working first, then evolving into this.

TOP-Nav reading guide — what to actually extract

You don't need every section equally. Here's what each part of the paper maps to in this architecture, in the order to read them.

Read in this order

  1. Figure 1 and the system diagram (likely Figure 2 or 3) — get the overall shape before reading any equations. Maps to: the overall three-layer shape of this document's architecture.
  2. The terrain estimator section — how do they build the traversability cost from sensor input? What's the network architecture, what's the training signal? Maps to: Layer 1 below — this is what makes your A*'s cost map "learned" instead of hand-coded.
  3. Equations 1-2 (the asymmetric actor-critic), which you've already read — confirm you understand what c_t is and why it can encode privileged information. Maps to: the input to Layer 3's feedback correction — whether you use a real c_t or your simplified proxy.
  4. The "proprioception advisor" section specifically — this is the one section worth reading slowly. Find the exact equation or rule for how c_t (or their motion-evaluation signal) updates the terrain/obstacle cost map. Is it an exponential moving average? A learned correction network? A simple additive term? Maps to: Layer 3 below, directly. This is the single most important mechanism to understand precisely, since it's the one your simplified proxy is standing in for.
  5. Skim the experiments section — you mainly want to know what failure modes they show their feedback loop fixing (e.g. does it help when vision misjudges terrain difficulty?). You don't need their exact numbers. Maps to: justifying, in your write-up, why the feedback loop matters at all — "this is the failure mode it's designed to catch."
What to write down after reading: one sentence on what their terrain estimator's input/output is, one sentence on the exact correction rule in the proprioception advisor, and one sentence on what real-world failure case the closed loop is shown to fix. If you can write those three sentences from memory without looking back at the paper, you've extracted what this document needs from TOP-Nav.

ABS reading guide — what to actually extract

Read in this order

  1. The system diagram showing the agile policy, recovery policy, and the value network together — get the shape of the switch before the math behind it. Maps to: Layer 2 below — this is the literal structure your dual-policy layer is a simplification of.
  2. How the agile policy's reward is defined — what does "agile" actually optimize for in their reward function? Speed? Tracking error? Something else? Maps to: your progress-policy's reward terms — should mirror this same prioritization.
  3. How the recovery policy's reward is defined, and what triggers a switch to it — is the switch a hard threshold, a probability, a continuous blend? Maps to: how your risk-prediction head's output should be used to blend or switch between your two policies — read this section closely, since it's the part you're simplifying away from a learned value network to a threshold.
  4. The reach-avoid value network section — what does it predict, what's its training signal, how is it different from a normal RL critic? Maps to: explicitly understanding what you're not building, so you can describe the simplification precisely rather than vaguely.
  5. Skim results showing agile-vs-recovery switching in action — look for a plot or trace showing when the switch fires during a real trajectory. Maps to: what a successful switch should look like when you validate your own version later.
What to write down after reading: one sentence each on the agile policy's reward, the recovery policy's reward, and how the reach-avoid value network decides to switch. Then one more sentence, explicitly: "my version replaces the reach-avoid value network with [your risk-prediction head's collision probability], which is simpler because [reason]." That last sentence is the one your supervisor will want to hear, almost verbatim.
Architecture

Full system, end to end

classical (unchanged) TOP-Nav derived ABS derived your RL, kept/split

Read the dashed teal line as the literal feedback loop from TOP-Nav: a signal computed from how the frozen locomotion policy is actually behaving (high effort, body tilting) flows back up and nudges the terrain cost used by layer 1's planner. The dashed orange lines are the ABS-derived switch: the risk head's output decides how much weight the progress policy versus the caution policy gets in the final blended command.

Layer 1 — terrain-aware global route

This is your existing A* planner, with one addition: instead of a hand-coded terrain cost (e.g. "rubble = high cost, flat ground = low cost"), a small learned network estimates traversability from sensor input, the way TOP-Nav's terrain estimator does. A* still runs once per goal over the static layout — nothing about A* itself changes, only what cost values it's planning over.

Why learn the terrain cost instead of keeping it hand-coded? A hand-coded cost only knows what you told it to know — "rubble is expensive" is true until you hit a kind of rough terrain you didn't anticipate. A learned estimator, trained on locomotion's own experience (this is exactly what layer 3 feeds back into it), can pick up terrain difficulty patterns you never explicitly coded, the same generalization argument that motivated implicit terrain adaptation over discrete terrain classification in your locomotion policy in the first place.

Layer 2 — dual progress / caution navigation policy

Instead of one PPO policy trying to be simultaneously fast and safe, train two smaller policies, each optimized for one objective, and blend their outputs using the risk-prediction head's collision-probability estimate as the blend weight. When predicted risk is low, the progress policy dominates the output; when risk is high, the caution policy takes over.

Why blend instead of a hard switch? A hard switch (100% progress policy, then suddenly 100% caution policy) can produce a visible jerk in the velocity command right at the switch point — exactly the kind of thing your jerk metric from phase 03 penalizes. A continuous blend, weighted by the risk head's probability output, gives a smoother transition and is simpler to implement than ABS's own value-network-gated switch, while keeping the same underlying idea: let a specialized policy take over when danger is anticipated, rather than asking one policy to handle both regimes adequately.
Why is this still "simplified ABS," not full ABS? ABS's reach-avoid value network is trained specifically to estimate reachability into unsafe states — a more rigorous, control-theoretic signal than a binary collision classifier. Your risk-prediction head is a simpler auxiliary output, already built in phase 02, repurposed here as the blend weight instead of training a new network. This is a legitimate scoped-down version, but say so explicitly rather than calling it ABS outright.

Layer 3 — closed-loop proprioception correction

This is unchanged from your existing design: the joint-torque-magnitude and base-orientation-deviation signal, read directly from the frozen locomotion policy's behavior, feeds back as an EMA correction to layer 1's terrain cost estimate. The only difference in this hybrid version is that it now corrects a learned terrain cost (layer 1) instead of a hand-coded one — so the correction has something more meaningful to adjust.

Why keep your proxy signal instead of switching to a real asymmetric critic value now? Your locomotion checkpoint is frozen and stable; retraining it with an asymmetric critic just to get a TOP-Nav-faithful c_t is a real cost for a feedback signal that's one piece of a now-larger system. Adding two more moving parts (dual policy, learned terrain cost) at the same time as retraining locomotion is a lot of simultaneous change to debug if something goes wrong. Keep the proxy here; revisit the asymmetric critic only if this whole hybrid system is working and you have time left for further refinement.

What's actually novel here

Be precise about this when you describe it to your supervisor: no single piece of this is new research. What's a genuine combination not present in any one paper is the specific pairing of a closed-loop, learned terrain cost (TOP-Nav's idea) feeding a classical planner, with a dual specialized navigation policy (ABS's idea) sitting downstream of that planner and using an anticipatory risk signal as a continuous blend weight rather than a hard switch. TOP-Nav has no learned navigation policy to combine with; ABS has no terrain-aware planner or feedback loop to combine with. The combination is the contribution, not any individual mechanism.

Recommended build order

Don't build all three layers' new pieces at once. A staged path that keeps you able to fall back to a working system at every step:

StepWhat changesWhy this order
0Your current single-policy nav (phases 1-3)Working baseline — never lose this
1Swap hand-coded terrain cost → learned terrain estimatorSmallest change, isolated to layer 1, easy to validate against the hand-coded baseline
2Split single nav policy → progress + caution policies, hard switch firstGet two policies training and producing sane output before adding blend complexity
3Hard switch → continuous blend using risk headRefinement once both policies independently work
4Re-point feedback loop at the new learned terrain costLast, since it depends on layer 1's new cost already being stable

If time runs out at any step, you still have a working, defensible system from the step before — this is the same "stage it so a partial result is still a result" logic from phase 04's multi-robot plan.

One-paragraph supervisor pitch

Say this, roughly

"Our navigation architecture combines ideas from two recent papers: from TOP-Nav, a learned terrain-cost estimator feeding a classical A* planner, closed by a proprioception feedback loop that corrects the cost map using the locomotion policy's own experience; from Agile But Safe, the idea of specializing navigation into a progress-seeking policy and a caution-biased policy rather than asking one policy to balance both, with an anticipatory risk signal blending between them. Neither paper combines these ideas — TOP-Nav has no learned navigation policy, and ABS has no terrain-aware planning or feedback loop — so the combination, scoped down to fit our timeline, is the contribution."
